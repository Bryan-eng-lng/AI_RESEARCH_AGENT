## SECTION: Introduction to Kodak: The Pioneer of Photography

Eastman Kodak, a name that has been synonymous with photography for over a century, has a rich and storied history that is marked by numerous innovations and groundbreaking achievements. Founded in 1880 by George Eastman, the company revolutionized the photography industry with its innovative products and technologies, including the introduction of the first roll film, the Brownie camera, and the iconic Kodachrome film [2]. At its peak, Kodak was the dominant player in the photography industry, with a market share of over 80% and annual revenues exceeding $10 billion [13]. The company's impact on the industry can be quantified by its impressive sales figures, with over 100 million cameras sold worldwide between 1970 and 1990, and a revenue growth rate of 10% per annum during the same period [14]. Furthermore, Kodak's market share in the film and camera market was a staggering 90% in the 1970s, making it the undisputed leader in the industry [15].

One of the most significant inventions in the company's history was the first self-contained digital camera, developed by Steven Sasson in 1975 [5]. This invention, which weighed around 8 pounds and captured images at a resolution of 100 x 100 pixels, was a groundbreaking achievement that would change the course of photography forever [5]. The development of the digital camera was a testament to Kodak's commitment to innovation and its willingness to invest in research and development. However, as we will explore in this section, Kodak's decision to not pursue the development of digital cameras at that time was a mistake that would ultimately lead to the company's downfall.

The 1970s were a time of great change and innovation in the photography industry. The introduction of new technologies, such as the charge-coupled device (CCD), made it possible to capture images electronically [6]. Sasson, a young engineer at Kodak, recognized the potential of this technology and developed the first digital camera using a CCD and a digital converter [7]. The digital camera was a significant innovation that had the potential to disrupt the traditional film-based camera market. However, despite the significance of this invention, Kodak did not see the potential for digital cameras to replace film-based cameras [3]. This decision was a mistake because it failed to recognize the disruptive potential of digital technology and the impact it would have on the photography industry.
